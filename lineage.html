How This Came To Be

I didn't start with Simmel. I didn't start with Moltmann. I started with frustration.

Years of watching organizations make the same mistakes:

  • Good people trapped in bad structures
  • One person carrying too much, burning out, leaving
  • Dissent going silent because it wasn't safe to speak
  • Succession becoming impossible because everything depended on one leader

I kept asking: What if we built it differently from the start?

Not "how do we fix this leader?" but "what structure makes this impossible?"

The answer kept pointing to three:

  • Three people, not one
  • Three voices, recorded
  • Three perspectives, required

So I built it. On paper. In my head. In conversations.

Then I did what you should always do: I researched.

And that's when I nearly stopped.

Because I kept finding pieces of what I'd built already existed:

  • Simmel wrote about triads in the 1890s
  • Moltmann, Volf, LaCugna developed Social Trinitarianism
  • Greenleaf published servant leadership in the 1970s
  • Supreme Courts record dissenting opinions
  • Distributed leadership research describes what works

My first thought: I'm not original. This is all borrowed.

My second thought: Maybe I should abandon this.

But then I realized something:

None of them put it together.

Simmel described triads — he didn't build decision procedures.
The Social Trinitarians wrote about relational theology — they didn't design organizational structures.
Greenleaf gave ethical guidance — he didn't enforce it architecturally.
Courts record dissent at the apex — not systematically at all levels.
Distributed leadership describes what happens — it doesn't prescribe the minimum viable unit.

The synthesis is new. The implementation is new. The combination is new.

So I didn't abandon it. I documented it. Honestly.

This page shows you what came before. What we inherited. What we added.

Because the innovation isn't in inventing components. It's in assembling them into something that works.

If you see gaps in this lineage — ideas we should acknowledge that we've missed — please contact us. Intellectual honesty is a core value of this framework.

Intellectual Honesty

Dynamic Trilateral Leadership does not claim to invent its component ideas. This framework stands on shoulders. The innovation is in the synthesis — combining existing insights into an implementable organizational system.

This page documents the lineage clearly so you can see:

  • What we inherited
  • What we added
  • Why the combination matters

Foundation 1: Triad Stability (Sociology)

Georg Simmel (1858-1918)

German sociologist, pioneer of microsociology

Key Work: The Sociology of Georg Simmel (1908), particularly the chapter on dyads and triads

What He Established:

  • Dyads (two-person groups) are inherently unstable — collapse when one person leaves
  • Triads (three-person groups) are the smallest stable social unit
  • Triads create structural possibilities dyads cannot: mediation, coalition, dissent
  • The third person transforms the group's dynamics fundamentally

What He Didn't Prescribe:

Simmel described triads. He didn't prescribe them as mandatory organizational structure. His work was analytical, not design-oriented.

What DTL Adds:

  • The triad as required minimum unit for all significant decisions
  • Operational rules for triad decision-making (2/3 voting, recorded dissent)
  • Dynamic authority within the triad (not just three equal votes)

Foundation 2: Social Trinitarianism (Theology)

Moltmann, Volf, LaCugna, Boff (20th century)

Protestant, Orthodox, and Catholic theologians

Key Works:

  • Moltmann, The Trinity and the Kingdom of God (1981)
  • Volf, After Our Likeness (1998)
  • LaCugna, God for Us: The Trinity and Christian Life (1991)
  • Boff, Trinity and Society (1988)

What They Established:

  • The Trinity is not just doctrine but a model for relationality
  • Three persons, distinct centers of consciousness, united in loving relationship
  • Human communities should reflect this divine pattern (perichoresis)
  • Unity without uniformity; distinction without division

What They Didn't Prescribe:

Social Trinitarians wrote about the Trinity as model for community. They didn't design specific organizational decision-making procedures.

What DTL Adds:

  • Operational mechanisms that mirror perichoresis (recorded dissent = distinction preserved in unity)
  • 2/3 voting as practical expression of "unity without uniformity"
  • Theological foundation made implementable

Foundation 3: Servant Leadership (Ethics)

Robert K. Greenleaf (1904-1990)

AT&T executive turned management consultant

Key Work: The Servant as Leader (1970)

What He Established:

  • Leaders should serve first, lead second
  • The best test: "Do those served grow as persons?"
  • Leadership as stewardship, not ownership

What He Didn't Prescribe:

Greenleaf's framework is ethical guidance without architectural enforcement. A "servant leader" can still be a solo decision-maker who happens to be kind about it.

What DTL Adds:

  • Structural constraints that make servant leadership inescapable
  • Three people, not one — power is distributed by design
  • Recorded dissent — the leader's view can be overruled
  • Dynamic authority — the "servant leader" doesn't always decide

Foundation 4: Distributed Leadership (Research)

Spillane, Gronn, and others (2000s-present)

Organizational and education researchers

Key Works:

  • Spillane, J. P. (2006). Distributed Leadership. Jossey-Bass.
  • Gronn, P. (2002). "Distributed leadership as a unit of analysis." Leadership Quarterly.

What They Established:

  • Leadership works best when stretched across multiple actors
  • "Leader plus" model — leadership activities distributed across the organization
  • Context matters — different situations need different leaders

What They Didn't Prescribe:

Distributed leadership research describes what happens in effective organizations. It doesn't prescribe the minimum viable unit (triad) or specific decision mechanisms.

What DTL Adds:

  • The triad as the atomic unit of distributed leadership
  • Specific mechanisms for distributing authority within the unit
  • Accountability structures that distributed leadership often lacks

Foundation 5: Recorded Dissent (Governance)

Supreme Courts, Corporate Boards, Parliamentary Systems

Established governance practice

Examples:

  • U.S. Supreme Court majority + dissenting opinions
  • UK House of Lords recorded votes
  • Some corporate boards (required for major decisions in certain jurisdictions)

What Exists:

Some institutions do record dissent. The practice is established in judicial systems and some governance contexts.

What's Missing:

Recorded dissent is typically limited to high-level decisions only, not systematic across all organizational levels, not paired with triadic structure or dynamic authority.

What DTL Adds:

  • Recorded dissent as default for all significant decisions
  • Dissent as institutional memory, not just historical record
  • Dissent tracking linked to authority shifts (vindicated dissent increases future authority)

The Synthesis: What's Actually New

Element Source DTL Innovation
Triad as stable unit Simmel (sociology) Mandatory for all significant decisions
Relational model Social Trinitarians (theology) Operational mechanisms (2/3 voting, recorded dissent)
Service ethic Greenleaf (ethics) Structural enforcement (can't hoard power)
Distributed authority Org research (management) Minimum unit specified (triad)
Recorded dissent Governance practice Systematic at all levels, linked to authority
AI advisory Emerging technology Permanent logged advisor, humans accountable
The innovation is the combination. Individually, none of these elements is new. Together, they create an organizational system that prevents benevolent dictatorship, preserves dissent intelligence, enables dynamic authority, and keeps AI in its proper role.

Why This Matters

For Christian organizations: The theological foundation is explicit. You can implement DTL as an expression of Trinitarian relationality and servant leadership.

For secular organizations: The sociological and management foundations stand on their own. You can implement DTL for structural stability without theological commitment.

For both: The framework is honest about its sources. You're not adopting someone's proprietary methodology. You're implementing a synthesis of established insights with new operational mechanisms.
